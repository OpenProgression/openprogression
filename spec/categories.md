# OpenProgression Categories

**Version:** 1.0.0

## Overview

OpenProgression assesses fitness across 8 categories. Together, these categories cover the full spectrum of functional fitness: strength, power, skill, endurance, and body control.

An athlete must demonstrate competency across **all 8 categories** to achieve a given OP level (the weakest-link principle).

---

## 1. Squatting

**Key Movements:** Back Squat, Front Squat, Overhead Squat

Lower body pushing strength through squat patterns. Tests bilateral leg strength, trunk stability, and mobility. The back squat is the primary benchmark as the most widely tested and researched squat variant.

**Why it matters:** Squatting strength is foundational to almost every functional movement. It correlates strongly with athletic performance across sports and is the single most predictive lift for overall strength.

**Benchmark type:** 1RM (one-rep max) in kilograms

---

## 2. Pulling

**Key Movements:** Deadlift, Sumo Deadlift, Romanian Deadlift

Hip-hinge and posterior chain strength. Tests the ability to move load from the floor through full hip extension. The conventional deadlift is the primary benchmark.

**Why it matters:** The deadlift tests total-body strength integration — grip, back, hips, and legs working together. It is the lift most representative of real-world strength (picking things up).

**Benchmark type:** 1RM in kilograms

---

## 3. Pressing

**Key Movements:** Strict Press, Push Press, Push Jerk, Bench Press

Upper body pressing strength in vertical and horizontal planes. The strict press (overhead) is the primary benchmark as it requires no leg drive and isolates pressing strength.

**Why it matters:** Pressing strength supports overhead stability, handstand movements, and the jerk portion of Olympic lifts. The bench press provides a horizontal pressing reference.

**Benchmark type:** 1RM in kilograms

---

## 4. Olympic Lifting

**Key Movements:** Power Clean, Power Snatch, Clean & Jerk, Snatch

Speed-strength and technical proficiency in the Olympic lifts. Tests explosive power, coordination, flexibility, and the ability to move load at high velocity. The clean & jerk and snatch are the primary benchmarks.

**Why it matters:** The Olympic lifts develop power output that transfers to sprinting, jumping, throwing, and fast-paced metabolic conditioning. They also require the highest degree of technical skill among barbell movements.

**Benchmark type:** 1RM in kilograms

---

## 5. Gymnastics

**Key Movements:** Strict Pull-up, Kipping Pull-up, Handstand Push-up, Toes-to-Bar, Bar Muscle-up, Ring Muscle-up

Bodyweight strength, control, and skill on apparatus. Progresses from basic pulling (pull-ups) through inversion (HSPU) to complex transitions (muscle-ups).

**Why it matters:** Gymnastics movements test relative strength (strength-to-bodyweight ratio), body awareness, and coordination. They are the domain where athletes most often hit their weakest-link limitation.

**Benchmark type:** Max unbroken reps

**Progression hierarchy:**
1. Strict Pull-up (baseline)
2. Toes-to-Bar (core + hip)
3. Handstand Push-up (pressing + inversion)
4. Bar Muscle-up (transition skill)
5. Ring Muscle-up (highest difficulty)

---

## 6. Monostructural

**Key Movements:** 500m Row, 2000m Row, 1-Mile Run, 5K Run

Single-modality cardiovascular output. Tests aerobic capacity, anaerobic threshold, and pacing across different time domains — from sprint (500m row, ~1:30) to sustained effort (5K run, ~20+ minutes).

**Why it matters:** Cardiovascular fitness is the foundation for recovery between efforts in any mixed-modal workout. It is also the clearest indicator of overall health and longevity.

**Benchmark type:** Time (lower is better)

---

## 7. Bodyweight

**Key Movements:** Push-up, Air Squat, Pistol Squat, Double-under, Rope Climb

Functional bodyweight strength and endurance without apparatus. Tests muscular endurance (push-ups), single-leg strength and balance (pistol squats), coordination (double-unders), and functional climbing.

**Why it matters:** Bodyweight competency is the most fundamental expression of fitness. These movements require no equipment and translate directly to everyday physical capability.

**Benchmark type:** Max reps or max unbroken reps

---

## 8. Endurance

**Key Movements (Benchmark WODs):** Fran, Grace, Murph, Cindy, Fight Gone Bad

Sustained performance across mixed-modal workouts of varying time domains. Tests the ability to maintain output when combining strength, gymnastics, and cardiovascular demands.

**Why it matters:** Functional fitness is ultimately expressed in the ability to perform varied tasks at sustained intensity. Benchmark workouts are the closest approximation to real-world fitness testing because they combine multiple domains under fatigue.

**Benchmark type:** Time (for-time workouts) or rounds/reps (AMRAPs)

**Benchmark WOD details:**
- **Fran** — 21-15-9: Thrusters (43/30kg) + Pull-ups. Tests power output under ascending fatigue. Time domain: 2-10 minutes.
- **Grace** — 30 Clean & Jerk for time (61/43kg). Tests Olympic lifting capacity under fatigue. Time domain: 1-8 minutes.
- **Murph** — 1-mile run, 100 pull-ups, 200 push-ups, 300 air squats, 1-mile run (with vest 9/6kg). Tests sustained bodyweight endurance. Time domain: 30-70 minutes.
- **Cindy** — 20-min AMRAP: 5 pull-ups, 10 push-ups, 15 air squats. Tests bodyweight work capacity over a fixed time domain.
- **Fight Gone Bad** — 3 rounds: wall balls, SDHP, box jumps, push press, row calories (1 min each, 1 min rest). Tests sustained power across multiple movements.

---

## Category Interaction

The 8 categories are designed to be **comprehensive but not redundant**. Each tests a distinct fitness quality:

| Quality | Primary Categories |
|---------|-------------------|
| Absolute strength | Squatting, Pulling, Pressing |
| Power / Speed-strength | Olympic Lifting |
| Relative strength | Gymnastics, Bodyweight |
| Cardiovascular capacity | Monostructural |
| Mixed-modal capacity | Endurance |

An athlete who excels in strength categories but neglects gymnastics and monostructural work will hit a ceiling — the weakest-link principle ensures this imbalance is reflected in their overall level.
