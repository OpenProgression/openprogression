# OpenProgression Levels

**Version:** 1.0.0

## Overview

OpenProgression uses a 7-level system to classify athletic ability. Each level represents a percentile range within the trained population, based on peer-reviewed normative data.

The system is designed to be:
- **Clear** — Level names are descriptive and intuitive
- **Progressive** — Each level requires meaningful improvement over the previous
- **Universal** — Applicable across all functional fitness categories

## Level Definitions

### Level 1: Beginner

- **Percentile range:** 0-20th
- **Training age:** 0-6 months
- **Color:** `#4ADE80` (green)

An individual new to structured fitness training. Learning fundamental movement patterns with minimal or no external load. Focus is on movement quality, range of motion, and building the habit of training.

**Characteristics:**
- Learning basic barbell movements (air squat, deadlift, press)
- Cannot perform strict pull-ups or other advanced gymnastics
- Developing cardiovascular base
- Requires coaching on every movement

### Level 2: Beginner+

- **Percentile range:** 20-35th
- **Training age:** 3-12 months
- **Color:** `#22C55E` (green)

Basic movements mastered with good form. Building consistency, work capacity, and confidence. Can follow a group class with minimal individual coaching.

**Characteristics:**
- Comfortable with all foundational barbell movements under light-to-moderate load
- May have first strict pull-up
- Can complete modified versions of benchmark workouts
- Starting to understand workout pacing

### Level 3: Intermediate

- **Percentile range:** 35-50th
- **Training age:** 1-2 years
- **Color:** `#EAB308` (yellow)

Solid fundamentals across all fitness categories. Handles moderate loads with consistently good form. Can participate in any class workout with standard scaling options.

**Characteristics:**
- Moderate strength relative to bodyweight (e.g., bodyweight back squat for males)
- Can perform strict pull-ups, kipping pull-ups, toes-to-bar
- Understands pacing for different workout time domains
- Can follow intermediate programming independently

### Level 4: Intermediate+

- **Percentile range:** 50-65th
- **Training age:** 2-3 years
- **Color:** `#F97316` (orange)

Competent athlete proficient across all fitness domains. Requires periodized programming for continued progress. Often serves as an example in class for movement quality.

**Characteristics:**
- Strong relative to bodyweight (1.3x BW back squat for males)
- Can perform handstand push-ups, chest-to-bar pull-ups
- Developing Olympic lift proficiency
- Can complete most benchmark workouts at prescribed weights
- Understands programming concepts (volume, intensity, recovery)

### Level 5: Advanced

- **Percentile range:** 65-80th
- **Training age:** 3-5 years
- **Color:** `#EF4444` (red)

Strong and skilled across all categories. Competition-capable at local level. Minimal weaknesses across the 8 assessment categories.

**Characteristics:**
- 1.6x+ BW back squat for males
- Can perform bar muscle-ups, ring muscle-ups
- Proficient Olympic lifter
- Completes all benchmark workouts at Rx weights
- Can coach movement patterns to less experienced athletes

### Level 6: Advanced+

- **Percentile range:** 80-95th
- **Training age:** 5+ years
- **Color:** `#DC2626` (dark red)

Very strong and highly skilled. Competition-level fitness across all domains. Performs at regional competition level.

**Characteristics:**
- 2x+ BW back squat for males
- Large unbroken sets of advanced gymnastics
- High-level Olympic lifting technique and loads
- Competes successfully in fitness competitions
- Can program for themselves and others

### Level 7: Rx

- **Percentile range:** 95-100th
- **Training age:** 5+ years of dedicated training
- **Color:** `#991B1B` (dark red)

Elite-level performance across all fitness domains. Top 5% of the trained population. Competition-ready at national or international level.

**Characteristics:**
- 2.3x+ BW back squat for males
- Rapid cycling of advanced gymnastics under fatigue
- Elite Olympic lifting numbers
- Can compete in high-level fitness competitions
- Exceptional work capacity across all time domains

## The Weakest-Link Principle

An athlete's **overall OP level** equals their **lowest category level**.

If an athlete tests at Advanced in 7 of 8 categories but Intermediate in one, their overall level is **Intermediate**. This design choice is intentional:

1. **Encourages well-rounded fitness** — Athletes cannot hide behind their strengths
2. **Identifies training priorities** — The lowest category is the obvious focus
3. **Reflects real-world performance** — In varied fitness testing, the weakest domain limits overall capability
4. **Motivates progression** — Bringing up a weakness immediately raises overall level

## Level Progression

Levels are not awarded through a single test or assessment. Instead, an athlete's level in each category is determined by their **personal records** compared against the benchmark standards.

- When an athlete logs a new PR that meets or exceeds the next level's benchmark, their category level updates
- All benchmarks for a level must be met to achieve that level in a category
- Partial progress toward the next level is tracked and visible
- Level history is preserved for long-term tracking

## Gender Differentiation

All benchmarks include separate standards for male and female athletes, derived from gender-specific normative data in the research literature. The 7-level percentile ranges are applied independently to male and female populations.

## Bodyweight Considerations

Barbell strength benchmarks in the standard are given as absolute values for a reference bodyweight (~80kg male / ~60kg female). For athletes significantly above or below these reference weights, proportional adjustment should be applied.

Future versions of the standard may include bodyweight-ratio-based benchmarks alongside absolute values.
